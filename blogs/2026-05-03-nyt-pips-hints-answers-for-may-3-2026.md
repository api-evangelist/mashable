However, we have you covered! Below are piecemeal answers that will serve as hints so that you can find your way through each difficulty level.</p><h2>How to play Pips</h2><p>If you've ever played dominoes, you'll have a passing familiarity with how <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/games/pips" target="_blank">Pips</a> is played. As we've shared in our previous hints stories for Pips, the tiles, like dominoes, are placed vertically or horizontally and connect with each other. The main difference between a traditional game of dominoes and Pips is the color-coded conditions you have to address. The touching tiles don't necessarily have to match.</p><div class="flex mx-auto mt-8 w-full max-w-3xl font-sans text-lg leading-normal md:text-xl md:leading-7">

<p>The conditions you have to meet are specific to the color-coded spaces. For example, if it provides a single number, every side of a tile in that space must add up to the number provided. It is possible &mdash; and common &mdash; for only half a tile to be within a color-coded space.</p><p>Here are common examples you'll run into across the difficulty levels:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Number</strong>: All the pips in this space must add up to the number.</p></li><li><p><strong>Equal</strong>: Every domino half in this space must be the same number of pips.</p></li><li><p><strong>Not Equal</strong>: Every domino half in this space must have a completely different number of pips.</p></li><li><p><strong>Less than</strong>: Every domino half in this space must add up to less than the number.</p></li><li><p><strong>Greater than:</strong> Every domino half in this space must add up to more than the number.</p></li></ul><p>If an area does not have any color coding, it means there are no conditions on the portions of dominoes within those spaces.</p><div class="flex mx-auto mt-8 w-full max-w-3xl font-sans text-lg leading-normal md:text-xl md:leading-7">
